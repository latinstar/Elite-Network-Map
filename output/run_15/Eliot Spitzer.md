[[Eliot Spitzer]] served as the 54th governor of [[New York]] and the 63rd attorney general of [[New York]], both as a member of the [[Democratic Party]]. As Attorney General, [[Eliot Spitzer]] gained a reputation as the "Sheriff of Wall Street" for his efforts to curb corruption in the financial services industry, often challenging traditional deference to the federal government. [[Eliot Spitzer]]'s political campaigns, including his runs for Attorney General and Governor, were significantly financed by his father, [[Bernard Spitzer]], through loans from [[J.P. Morgan & Company]], a practice that drew scrutiny from the [[New York State Senate Investigations Committee]]. [[Eliot Spitzer]] worked as a prosecutor for the [[New York County District Attorney's office]] under [[Robert Morgenthau]], where he notably led an investigation that ended the [[Gambino crime family]]'s control of Manhattan's trucking and garment industries. During his tenure as Attorney General, [[Eliot Spitzer]] hired [[Alvin Bragg]] as an Assistant Attorney General, indicating his influence on future legal leadership within the state. [[Eliot Spitzer]]'s gubernatorial campaign was endorsed by prominent [[Democratic Party]] figures like [[Alan Hevesi]], [[David Dinkins]], and [[Ed Koch]], and he was dubbed the "future of the [[Democratic Party]]" by [[Bill Richardson]]. As Governor, [[Eliot Spitzer]] frequently clashed with the [[New York State Legislature]] and individual assemblymen like [[William B. Magnarelli]], [[George S. Latimer]], and Minority Leader [[James Tedisco]] over appointments and policy, earning him a reputation as a "steamroller." [[Eliot Spitzer]]'s administration, specifically his communications director [[Darren Dopp]], was investigated by [[Andrew Cuomo]]'s office and his own [[Inspector General, Kristine Hamann]], for using [[State Police]] resources to gather and release information about [[State Senate]] Majority Leader [[Joe Bruno]] in the "Troopergate" controversy. [[Eliot Spitzer]]'s executive order allowing driver's licenses for illegal immigrants was challenged by the [[Department of Homeland Security]] and ultimately overturned by the [[State Senate]], demonstrating a limit to his executive power. [[Eliot Spitzer]] resigned from the governorship following a prostitution scandal involving the high-priced escort service [[Emperors Club VIP]], which was uncovered by federal wiretaps. After leaving office, [[Eliot Spitzer]] continued to engage in real estate activity and private investments, and unsuccessfully ran for [[New York City Comptroller]] against [[Scott Stringer]].
