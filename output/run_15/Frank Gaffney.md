[[Frank Gaffney]] founded the influential national security think tank, the [[Center for Security Policy (CSP)]], in 1988, serving as its president until 2023 and now as executive chairman.
Earlier in his career, [[Frank Gaffney]] served in the federal government, including as [[Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense for Nuclear Forces and Arms Control Policy]] and [[Acting Assistant Secretary of Defense for International Security Affairs]] during the [[Reagan administration]], where he worked under [[Richard Perle]] and previously as an aide to Senator [[Henry M. Jackson]].
Despite his government service, [[Frank Gaffney]] was forced out of the Pentagon and became a vocal critic of [[Ronald Reagan]]'s arms control agreements with the [[Soviet Union]].
The [[Center for Security Policy (CSP)]], under [[Frank Gaffney]]'s leadership, focuses on perceived jihadist threats to the [[United States]] and has been classified as a "hate group" by the [[Southern Poverty Law Center (SPLC)]], which describes [[Frank Gaffney]] as "one of Americaâ€™s most notorious Islamophobes."
[[Frank Gaffney]] and the [[Center for Security Policy (CSP)]] are recognized as influential components of the [[counter-jihad movement]].
His influence extended to presidential campaigns, with [[Ted Cruz]] announcing [[Frank Gaffney]] as a [[National Security Advisor]] in 2016, and [[Donald Trump]] citing a [[Center for Security Policy (CSP)]]-funded poll while appearing to build an anti-Muslim policy framework mirroring [[Gaffney]]'s proposals.
Within the [[Trump administration]], [[Frank Gaffney]] and the [[Center for Security Policy (CSP)]] notably influenced figures like [[Michael Flynn]], [[Mike Pompeo]], [[Jeff Sessions]], and [[John Bolton]], whose anti-Islamic and [[Iran nuclear deal]] beliefs were criticized as originating from [[Gaffney]].
[[Frank Gaffney]] regularly appeared on [[Steve Bannon]]'s radio show during his time at [[Breitbart News]], further demonstrating his media reach.
Ideologically aligned, [[Frank Gaffney]] was identified by Professor [[Todd Green]] alongside [[David Yerushalmi]], the former legal counsel for the [[Center for Security Policy (CSP)]], for spreading misinformation and seeking anti-Muslim laws.
[[Frank Gaffney]] is a prolific media contributor, writing for [[Newsmax]], [[The Washington Times]], and [[Jewish World Review]], and hosted [[Secure Freedom Radio]], a program that featured prominent guests such as [[Newt Gingrich]], [[John R. Bolton]], and white nationalist [[Jared Taylor]], which later became [[Securing America TV]] on [[Real America's Voice]].
He also serves as vice-chair of the [[Committee on the Present Danger]] and is associated with a "new red scare" of anti-Chinese sentiment.
[[Frank Gaffney]] has employed tactics like "fax wars" to influence policy debates and target figures such as [[US Secretary of Energy Hazel R. O'Leary]] and the [[Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty (CTBT)]] with "intensely personal attacks" and numerous "screeds."
The [[Anti-Defamation League (ADL)]] asserts that [[Frank Gaffney]] "has promulgated a number of anti-Muslim conspiracy theories" and wields "undue influence."
His prominent "Civilization Jihad" conspiracy theory, alleging a secret Muslim plan to take over America, was cited by 2016 presidential candidate [[Ben Carson]], despite being based on a discredited source that the [[FBI]] found incongruous and the [[BRIDGE Initiative at Georgetown]] noted failed to gain traction in the Arab world.
Despite being banned from the [[Conservative Political Action Conference (CPAC)]] in 2011 by [[American Conservative Union (ACU)]] chairman [[David Keene]] for his extreme views, [[Frank Gaffney]] later returned to host panels at the conference.
