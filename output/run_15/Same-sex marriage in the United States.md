The legal recognition of [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]] advanced through a complex interplay of judicial rulings, legislative actions, and shifts in public opinion.
Initially, the [[Supreme Court of the United States]] declined to intervene in the issue in 1972 with [[Baker v. Nelson]].
Early judicial influence emerged from the [[Supreme Court of Hawaii]] in [[Baehr v. Lewin]] (1993), which ruled that abridging marriage based on sex was unconstitutional.
In response to these early judicial pushes for [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]], [[Congress]] enacted the [[Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA)]] in 1996, strategically restricting federal recognition of same-sex unions.
The [[Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court]] further propelled the movement for [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]] in 2003 with [[Goodridge v. Department of Public Health]], leading Massachusetts to become the first state to legalize it.
[[Human rights and civil rights organizations]] served as prominent supporters of [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]], advocating for its recognition and shaping public discourse.
The [[NAACP]], a leading African-American civil rights organization, declared its support for [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]] as a civil right in 2012, aligning it with broader civil rights movements and exerting ideological influence.
Prominent civil rights figures such as [[Coretta Scott King]], [[John Lewis]], [[Julian Bond]], and [[Mildred Loving]] lent their moral authority and influence to the movement for [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]].
[[Religious groups]] represented the most prominent opponents of [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]], exerting ideological opposition, though some religious organizations supported marriage equality.
The [[Supreme Court of the United States]] significantly shaped the legal landscape of [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]] by striking down [[Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA)]] in [[United States v. Windsor]] (2013), leading to federal recognition and benefits.
The landmark ruling of [[Obergefell v. Hodges]] (2015) by the [[Supreme Court of the United States]] legalized [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]] nationwide, citing the [[Due Process Clause]] and [[Equal Protection Clause]] of the [[Fourteenth Amendment]] to establish a fundamental right.
After [[Obergefell v. Hodges]], [[Justice Clarence Thomas]] later indicated an ongoing ideological challenge to [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]] by naming the case as one that should be revisited in his concurrence with [[Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization]].
To protect [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]] from potential future challenges, [[Congress]] passed the [[Respect for Marriage Act]] in 2022, which was signed into law by [[President Joe Biden]], reflecting legislative power to codify rights.
The [[Respect for Marriage Act]] explicitly nullified the remnants of the [[Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA)]] and federally protected [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]], demonstrating a shift in federal legislative power.
[[Roy Moore]], as Alabama's Chief Justice, acted as a controversial political operative by issuing a ruling in 2016 attempting to forbid state officials from issuing marriage licenses for [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]], which directly challenged federal authority and led to his suspension for ethics violations.
The [[Justice Department]] expanded federal recognition and benefits for [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]] following the [[United States v. Windsor]] and [[Obergefell v. Hodges]] decisions, demonstrating executive branch influence in implementation.
The [[Alabama House of Representatives]] exerted ideological influence by adopting a resolution in 2014 calling for a constitutional convention to propose an amendment banning [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]] nationwide, reflecting ongoing state-level opposition.
Professional organizations such as the [[American Psychological Association]] and [[American Medical Association]] implicitly support [[Same-sex marriage in the United States]] through their mentioned advocacy for recognition, contributing expert and institutional influence to the network of support.
