[[The New York Times]] is published by [[The New York Times Company]], which has been chaired by the [[Ochs-Sulzberger family]] since 1896, demonstrating entrenched familial control and influence over the newspaper.
This control is solidified by the [[Ochs-Sulzberger family]]'s majority ownership of [[The New York Times Company]] through a dual-class stock structure, allowing them to elect 70% of the board of directors and ensuring their strategic influence.
Historically, [[The New York Times]] exerted political influence by aggressively covering and exposing corrupt figures like [[Boss Tweed]] and [[William M. Tweed]] of [[Tammany Hall]] in the 1870s.
[[The New York Times]] has played a pivotal role in shaping legal precedents for media freedom, notably through the landmark [[New York Times Co. v. Sullivan]] (1964) case and the [[Supreme Court]] ruling in its favor in [[New York Times Co. v. United States (1971)]] for publishing the [[Pentagon Papers]] despite opposition from President [[Richard Nixon]] and the [[Department of Defense]].
Its involvement with national security operations includes journalist [[William L. Laurence]] being recruited by the [[United States government]] to document the secret [[Manhattan Project]] during [[World War II]], leading to the [[Federal Bureau of Investigation]] seizing copies of the Times due to Laurence's earlier reporting on the atomic bomb race.
[[The New York Times]] faced government scrutiny during the McCarthy era from the [[Senate Internal Security Subcommittee]] and experienced internal conflicts related to an employee pleading the [[Fifth Amendment]], indicating political pressures on its editorial independence.
In the lead-up to the [[Iraq War]], an article by [[Judith Miller]] and [[Michael R. Gordon]] in [[The New York Times]] claiming [[Iraq]]'s purchase of aluminum tubes was cited by President [[George W. Bush]] as justification, demonstrating its direct influence on major foreign policy decisions based on potentially speculative intelligence.
Further demonstrating entanglement with intelligence and political operatives, a [[The New York Times]] article disclosing warrantless [[National Security Agency]] surveillance influenced political debate and the renewal of the [[Patriot Act]], while [[Judith Miller]]'s role in the [[Plame affair]] connected her and the paper to a [[Central Intelligence Agency]] inquiry and high-level political operative [[Scooter Libby]], ultimately leading to Miller's resignation.
During the [[Great Recession]], [[The New York Times Company]] borrowed $250 million from Mexican billionaire [[Carlos Slim]], highlighting its reliance on elite external financing to navigate significant financial crises.
[[The New York Times]] exerted influence on contemporary political events by elevating the [[Hillary Clinton email controversy]] before the [[2016 presidential election]] and by providing a platform for dissent through publishing an anonymous essay, "[[I Am Part of the Resistance Inside the Trump Administration]]", later revealed to be from then-[[Department of Homeland Security]] chief of staff [[Miles Taylor]].
[[The New York Times]] developed an adversarial relationship with former President [[Donald Trump]], who labeled it "enemies of the people" and ordered federal agencies to cancel subscriptions, showcasing a direct conflict between the press and the executive branch.
[[The New York Times]] has demonstrated significant influence on social movements, notably through the investigation into [[Harvey Weinstein]] by [[Jodi Kantor]] and [[Megan Twohey]], which served as a catalyst for the [[#MeToo movement]].
Its digital platform, nytimes.com, gained legitimacy and influenced state-level politics through its coverage of the [[Eliot Spitzer prostitution scandal]], which led to the resignation of then-New York governor [[Eliot Spitzer]].
[[The New York Times Company]] also holds undisclosed minority investments in various other businesses, suggesting a broader network of financial influence extending beyond its primary media operations.
A 2025 [[Pew Research Center]] study found that [[The New York Times]] attracts a disproportionately college-educated readership, indicating its influence over a specific and often politically engaged demographic.
