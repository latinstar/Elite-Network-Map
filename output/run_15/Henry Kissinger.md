[[Henry Kissinger]] served as the [[United States secretary of state]] and [[National Security Advisor]] under [[President Richard Nixon]] and [[President Gerald Ford]], establishing himself as a central figure in U.S. foreign policy. Before his government appointments, [[Henry Kissinger]] acted as a consultant to various [[government agencies]], [[think tanks]], and the presidential campaigns of [[Nelson Rockefeller]] and [[Richard Nixon]]. [[Henry Kissinger]] pioneered the policy of [[détente]] with the [[Soviet Union]] and orchestrated an opening of relations with [[China]], leading to a new strategic anti-Soviet alignment. He engaged in "shuttle diplomacy" in the [[Middle East]] to end the [[Yom Kippur War]] and negotiated the [[Paris Peace Accords]], for which he was controversially awarded the [[Nobel Peace Prize]]. [[Henry Kissinger]] is associated with controversial U.S. policies including the bombing of [[Cambodia]], involvement in the [[1971 Bolivian coup d'état]] and [[1973 Chilean coup d'état]], and support for [[Argentina's military junta]] during its Dirty War, [[Indonesia]] in its invasion of East Timor, and [[Pakistan]] during the [[Bangladesh Liberation War]]. After leaving government, [[Henry Kissinger]] founded [[Kissinger Associates]], an international geopolitical consulting firm, through which he continued to exert influence until his death. The advice of [[Henry Kissinger]] was sought by [[American presidents]] of both major political parties, demonstrating his enduring political relevance. While a graduate student at [[Harvard University]], [[Henry Kissinger]] consulted for the [[Psychological Strategy Board]] and notably sought to work as a spy for the [[FBI]]. [[Henry Kissinger]] directed the [[Harvard International Seminar]] and later the [[Harvard Defense Studies Program]], serving as a key academic leader influencing defense and foreign policy discourse. He worked for the [[Rockefeller Brothers Fund]] as director of its Special Studies Project and co-founded the [[Center for International Affairs]] with [[Robert R. Bowie]]. [[Henry Kissinger]] served as a study director for nuclear weapons and foreign policy at the [[Council on Foreign Relations]], publishing a book that criticized the [[Eisenhower administration]]'s nuclear doctrine. The close relationship between [[Henry Kissinger]] and [[President Richard Nixon]] facilitated numerous "backchannel" negotiations, often excluding the [[Department of State]] and involving figures like [[Soviet Ambassador Anatoly Dobrynin]]. [[Henry Kissinger]] worked directly with [[Premier Zhou Enlai]] during his secret trips to the [[People's Republic of China]], addressing the status of [[Taiwan]] and ultimately paving the way for the normalization of relations between the two countries. Despite earlier promises to [[Chiang Ching-kuo]] not to abandon [[Taiwan]], [[Henry Kissinger]] implicitly accepted Taiwan's expulsion from the [[United Nations]] by the [[United Nations General Assembly]], effectively working against the "two Chinas" efforts of [[American Ambassador George H. W. Bush]]. Prior to his official appointment as National Security Advisor, [[Henry Kissinger]] was a consultant to [[Henry Cabot Lodge Jr.]] regarding [[Indochina]] and served as an intermediary for negotiations between the [[United States]] and [[North Vietnam]].
