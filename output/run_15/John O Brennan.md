[[John O. Brennan]] served as [[CIA Director]] from 2013 to 2017, having been nominated twice by [[U.S. President Barack Obama]]. Prior to this, [[John O. Brennan]] was a chief counterterrorism advisor to [[U.S. President Barack Obama]], holding the titles of [[Deputy National Security Advisor for Homeland Security and Counterterrorism]] and [[Assistant to the President]], and advised [[Obama]] during his 2008 presidential campaign and transition. [[John O. Brennan]] spent 25 years with the [[CIA]], working as a Near East and South Asia analyst, station chief in [[Saudi Arabia]], daily intelligence briefer for [[President Bill Clinton]], chief of staff to [[George Tenet]], deputy executive director, and director of the [[National Counterterrorism Center]] and the [[Terrorist Threat Integration Center]]. After leaving government in 2005, [[John O. Brennan]] became [[CEO]] of [[The Analysis Corporation]], a security consulting business, and chairman of the [[Intelligence and National Security Alliance (INSA)]], later leading [[The Analysis Corporation]] after its acquisition by [[Global Strategies Group]] (which included [[GTEC]]). As a key figure in the [[Obama administration]], [[John O. Brennan]] was the first official to publicly acknowledge [[CIA drone strikes]] and was described as the "principal coordinator" of [[U.S. kill lists]], helping to reorganize the [[Disposition Matrix]] to concentrate power within the [[White House administration]]. [[John O. Brennan]] faced scrutiny from the [[ACLU]] and a [[Senate filibuster]] by [[Senator Rand Paul]] regarding his role in drone strikes and past support for [[enhanced interrogation]] and [[extraordinary rendition]], also being compared to [[Omar Khadr]] by [[Morris Davis]] for his role in missile strikes. During his tenure as [[CIA Director]], [[John O. Brennan]] created ten new [[CIA mission centers]] and the [[Directorate for Digital Innovation (DDI)]] to focus on cybersecurity, though some [[CIA]] officials expressed reservations about moving away from human intelligence. [[John O. Brennan]] visited [[Kyiv]] where he met with [[Ukrainian Prime Minister Arseniy Yatsenyuk]] and [[First Deputy Prime Minister Vitaliy Yarema]] to purportedly discuss intelligence-sharing between the [[United States]] and [[Ukraine]]. Upon leaving office, [[John O. Brennan]] became a senior national security and intelligence analyst for [[NBC News]] and [[MSNBC]] and a consultant for [[Kissinger Associates]], also serving as a [[Distinguished Non-Resident Scholar]] and senior advisor to the [[Intelligence Studies Project]] at [[The University of Texas at Austin]]. [[John O. Brennan]] was a target of cyberattacks, including the hacking of his personal [[AOL]] email account, which was subsequently posted on [[WikiLeaks]], and a separate incident where British security hacker [[Kane Gamble]] posed as him and accessed his private accounts, with North Carolina residents [[Justin Gray Liverman]] and [[Andrew Otto Boggs]] later pleading guilty in connection with a group called '[[Crackas With Attitude]]'. [[John O. Brennan]] briefed [[President-elect Donald Trump]] (alongside [[FBI Director James Comey]], [[NSA Director Mike Rogers]], and [[Director of National Intelligence James Clapper]]) on [[Russian election interference]] and the [[Steele dossier]]. Since the 2016 election, [[John O. Brennan]] has been a vocal critic of [[President Donald Trump]], leading to [[Trump]] announcing the revocation of Brennan's security clearance, and [[Brennan]] helped establish the [[FBI]]'s counterintelligence investigation into [[Donald Trump]]'s campaign.
